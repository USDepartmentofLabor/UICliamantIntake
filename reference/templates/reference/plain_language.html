When applicants don't understand a question on the unemployment insurance (UI) application (paper or online form), it often leads to inaccurate responses that can delay eligibility determinations and payment. Complicated questions also creates an equity issue, giving an unfair advantage to claimants with advanced reading and English fluency levels. Leverage the examples below to learn about plain language best practices, and to inspire improvements in your own state or territory's unemployment application questions.

Define complex terms

Many English words or terms do not have direct or intuitive translations to different languages. Many UI terms such as "laid off," "fired," and "quit," may have different nuances depending on the states and territories. In the UI application context, it is important that applicants understand UI terminology and concepts so they can make an accurate selection. Without clear definitions or additional context, applicants who speak English as a second language, or who don't correctly interpret what a term means for the state in question, may face insurmountable barriers to applying, or may submit an inaccurate response that SWAs must investigate and correct. This creates burdens not only for the applicants, but also for SWA staff.

When possible, provide short, clear definitions on the same page as the question being asked.

Separation
What was your reason for separation?

What we learned from testing

When applicants were shown definitions, they slowed down, read this screen more carefully, and chose the correct answer, leading us to believe that this would also result in submissions that are easier and faster for SWA staff to process.

"I like the explanations here because if it just said laid off, fired, etc. I would not understand it." - UI Applicant

Use simpler terms when possible

There are tools and resources available for free that can analyze web content for readability. Word processing applications can also assess the grade level of your website or form content. If content is above grade 8, make edits to simplify the language. Small changes can remove barriers to access and reduce errors in applications.

Break up long sentences

Previously, the attestation sentence before the signature caused confusion. Splitting this sentence into two sentences without changing the content brought the reading level down to grade 8.

I certify that my responses to the preceding questions are true. I am aware that the law imposes penalties for false answers.
B
Break a long sentence into two, use bullets, eliminate passive voice and unnecessary words, and edit sentences that are hard to read.

Include helper text

People applying for unemployment insurance may be experiencing stress and anxiety as a result of their recent unemployment. When they are confronted with questions they don’t know how to answer, those feelings are compounded by their concern that an incorrect choice will lead to their application being rejected or denied.

"Helper text" can supplement existing information or questions on the application, giving applicants more context or examples so they can provide the most relevant and accurate information.

Your demographic information

We ask for your demographic information only for our reporting requirements. The information you share does not affect your application or your benefit amount.

C
Applicants felt more comfortable providing information when they understood why a question was being asked, and how the information would be used.

Confirm address

Which address do you want to use for your primary address?
D
Helper text can guide applicants to a more efficient or easier option.

Choosing the U.S. Postal Service recommendation ensures that it meets the postal address standards. Unless it's wrong, we suggest using this to avoid mail delays. Learn more here.

Your most recent employer:

You can usually find your employer or company's name on your paystub or W2.

E
Helper text can provide a hint if the applicant is unsure of where or how to find information.

Education and training

Your education
Are you currently attending school, college, or job training?

If you are in union-required training or paid job training, choose "No."

Vocational rehabilitation
Are you currently registered with Vocational Rehabilitation?

Vocational Rehabilitation is a program that helps people living with disabilities find and keep jobs. Learn more here.

F
